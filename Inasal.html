My mother’s name, Jennylyn, is derived from Jenny, meaning "fair one," and Lyn, meaning "lake." My father’s name, Julius, came from a Latin word meaning "youthful." Names carry weight. They hold echoes of history, culture, and identity—and mine is no exception. My full name, Mark Dustin Bolaño, is more than a simple arrangement of syllables. It's a reflection of myth, strength, and legacy.
The name Mark traces back to Marcus, which itself is derived from Mars, the Roman God of War. In ancient mythology, Mars wasn’t merely a symbol of violence—he represented discipline, resilience, and the protective strength of a soldier. As the father of Romulus and Remus, the legendary founders of Rome, Mars was also tied to the birth of civilization itself. That connection has always made me feel as though I carry, in some small way, the spirit of a warrior—one who stands firm in the face of adversity, grounded by purpose and determination.
Dustin, my second name, has roots in the Old Norse name Thorstien, meaning “Thor’s Stone.” Thor—the god of thunder, lightning, and storms—was the defender of Asgard, wielding his hammer Mjölnir with unmatched force. The idea of being linked to Thor’s stone, a symbol of raw power and divine protection, reminds me of endurance. It’s not just about strength, but about standing tall during the storm—weathering trials with resilience and inner fortitude.
My surname, Bolaño, offers yet another layer of depth. Historically, "bolaño" means cannonballs—objects of war, used in infantry and maritime battles during the era of buccaneers and privateers. It calls to mind thundering ships, gunpowder smoke, and the calculated chaos of battle at sea. The name is bold, forceful, and unflinching. But there's also a literary dimension to it. I share this name with Roberto Bolaño, the brilliant Chilean novelist whose works explore exile, resistance, and the fragmented search for meaning. His voice brought power to the page, proving that not all battles are fought with steel—some are fought with stories, memory, and words.

Chapter 2: Portfolio

When I first set out to build this portfolio, I had a clear vision in mind. I wanted a seamless, modern experience—something with a sticky feature that would allow the content to flow within a single HTML page. My goal was to create an interactive digital copy, smooth in its transitions, where every scroll felt intentional and cohesive.
But as I began translating my idea into code, I quickly realized that implementing advanced transitions and sticky behavior between multiple sections on a single HTML file was far more complex than I anticipated. Especially for someone still early in their web development journey, the deeper I dove into the syntax and structure, the more challenging it became to maintain consistency across my content without breaking the flow or running into layout bugs.
So I decided to improvise.

Media

Layout

Rather than trying to force everything into one file, I split the website into two separate HTML files. The first HTML now serves as the container for my digital copy’s header—a clean introduction that presents my name, navigation links, and initial design elements. The second HTML file houses the entire body of the portfolio—the content-rich sections like my story, portfolio chapters, and contact information.
